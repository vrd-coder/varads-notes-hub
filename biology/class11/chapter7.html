Chapter 7: Locomotion and Movement

🔰 Introduction

Movement = A fundamental property of living organisms.

It includes internal movements (heartbeat, cytoplasmic streaming) and external movements (limb motion).

Locomotion = Movement resulting in change of place (e.g., walking, flying).

✅ All locomotions are movements, but not all movements are locomotion.

Examples:

  • WBC moving = Movement only
  • Man walking = Movement + Locomotion

Purpose:

  • 🔹 Food
  • 🔹 Shelter
  • 🔹 Mating
  • 🔹 Escape from enemies
  • 🔹 Adaptation

🔹 7.1 Types of Movement

  1. 🧬 Amoeboid Movement
    • Seen in: WBCs, macrophages, Amoeba
    • Uses pseudopodia (false feet)
  2. 🧹 Ciliary Movement
    • Seen in: Trachea (respiratory tract), oviduct
    • Moves mucus, ovum, etc.
  3. 💪 Muscular Movement
    • Most common in higher animals
    • Movement via contraction of muscles

🔹 7.2 Muscles

Muscles form ~40–50% of body weight.

Function: Movement, support, posture.

Type of Muscle Control Striated Shape Nucleus Location
🏋️‍♂️ Skeletal Voluntary Yes Cylindrical Multinucleated Attached to bones
🫀 Visceral (Smooth) Involuntary No Spindle Single nucleus Walls of organs
❤️ Cardiac Involuntary Yes Branched Single nucleus Only in heart

🔹 7.2.1 Structure of Skeletal Muscle

Skeletal muscle → muscle bundles → muscle fibres → myofibrils

Each fibre = cylindrical, multinucleated, enclosed by sarcolemma

Cytoplasm = Sarcoplasm, contains:

  • ✅ Myofibrils (contractile)
  • ✅ Mitochondria
  • ✅ Sarcoplasmic reticulum (stores Ca²⁺)

🔹 7.3 Skeletal Muscle: Contractile Proteins

🔬 Key Proteins:

  1. Actin (thin filament)
    • F-actin (filamentous), Troponin, Tropomyosin
    • Troponin binds Ca²⁺
    • Tropomyosin covers binding sites
  2. Myosin (thick filament)
    • Has a globular head with ATPase activity
    • Forms cross-bridge with actin

🔹 7.4 Mechanism of Muscle Contraction

🧠 Sliding Filament Theory (by Huxley & Niedergerke, 1954)

🧾 Steps:

  1. Motor neuron releases acetylcholine → action potential on sarcolemma
  2. Ca²⁺ released from sarcoplasmic reticulum
  3. Ca²⁺ binds troponin, exposes actin’s binding site
  4. Myosin head binds to actin → forms cross-bridge
  5. ATP is hydrolyzed → myosin head bends → actin slides inward → contraction
  6. Ca²⁺ reabsorbed → tropomyosin covers site again → relaxation

⚠️ A-band remains same, I-band shortens, H-zone disappears, sarcomere shortens

🔹 7.4.1 Muscle Fatigue

Occurs due to:

  • • ATP depletion
  • • Accumulation of lactic acid (anaerobic respiration)

Muscle temporarily loses ability to contract

🔹 7.4.2 Red and White Muscle Fibres

Feature 🔴 Red Muscle Fibres ⚪ White Muscle Fibres
Myoglobin High Low
Mitochondria Many Few
Contraction Slow, sustained Fast, brief
Fatigue Resistant Fatigue quickly
Example Postural muscles (back) Eye, hand muscles

🔹 7.5 Skeletal System

Provides support, shape, protection, locomotion

Made up of bones + cartilages

Total bones in adult: 206

🔹 7.5.1 Axial Skeleton (80 bones)

Includes:

  1. Skull (22 bones) – 8 cranial, 14 facial
  2. Vertebral Column (26 vertebrae)
    • Cervical (7)
    • Thoracic (12)
    • Lumbar (5)
    • Sacral (1 fused)
    • Coccygeal (1 fused)
  3. Sternum – Breastbone
  4. Ribs (12 pairs)
    • True ribs (1–7)
    • False ribs (8–10)
    • Floating ribs (11–12)

🔹 7.5.2 Appendicular Skeleton (126 bones)

Includes:

  • Limb Bones:
    • Forelimbs (arms) – 30 × 2
    • Hindlimbs (legs) – 30 × 2
  • Girdles:
    • Pectoral Girdle (2 clavicles + 2 scapulae)
    • Pelvic Girdle (2 hip bones – ilium, ischium, pubis fused)

🔹 7.6 Joints

A joint is a junction between two or more bones.

Types of Joints:

Type Mobility Example
🔗 Fibrous Immovable Skull sutures
📏 Cartilaginous Slightly movable Intervertebral discs
⚙️ Synovial Freely movable Knee, shoulder

🔹 Types of Synovial Joints

Type Example
⚽ Ball & Socket Shoulder, Hip
🧲 Hinge Elbow, Knee
🔄 Pivot Atlas-Axis (neck)
📐 Gliding Between carpals
👍 Saddle Thumb joint (carpo-metacarpal)

🔹 Common Muscular & Skeletal Disorders

  1. Myasthenia Gravis
    • Autoimmune
    • Causes weakness in skeletal muscles
  2. Muscular Dystrophy
    • Genetic disorder
    • Progressive degeneration of muscles
  3. Arthritis
    • Inflammation of joints
    • Pain, swelling, stiffness
  4. Osteoporosis
    • Loss of bone density
    • Common in elderly, post-menopausal women

📚 Summary – NCERT

  1. Movement is a vital feature of living organisms.
  2. Locomotion involves a change in location using muscles.
  3. Muscles are of 3 types: skeletal, smooth, and cardiac.
  4. Skeletal muscles have myofibrils made of actin and myosin.
  5. Muscle contraction follows the sliding filament theory.
  6. Red and white fibres differ in endurance and speed.
  7. The skeletal system provides support and protection.
  8. Axial skeleton includes skull, ribs, vertebral column.
  9. Appendicular skeleton includes limbs and girdles.
  10. Joints are classified as fibrous, cartilaginous, and synovial.
  11. Common disorders include arthritis, osteoporosis, myasthenia gravis, etc.
